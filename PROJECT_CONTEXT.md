# Project Context: Team Protection & Escalation Builder

## Working title

Team Protection & Escalation Builder

Alternative German title:

Schutz- und Eskalationsmodell für sensible Teams

---

## Core idea

This is a compact guided HTML tool that helps teams co-create an escalation model for sensitive situations.

It is especially designed for university contexts such as:

- ethics committees
- research groups
- professor-led teams
- governance bodies
- confidential employee-protection situations
- settings with power asymmetries

The goal is not to create a compliance policy.

The goal is to help a real team build a usable model for:

- when to speak up
- when to seek protected advice
- when to escalate formally
- when to activate independent or protective channels
- how to de-escalate and return to normal work

---

## Strategic framing

Avoid making escalation sound like punishment, control, or failure.

Preferred framing:

> Escalation is a protection path.

Core message:

> In sensitive committees, escalation is not a sign of failure. It is a pre-agreed protection path when people, roles, or decisions come under pressure.

This is especially important in contexts involving:

- professors
- junior researchers
- administrative staff
- hierarchical dependencies
- reputational risk
- confidential cases
- ethical concerns
- fear of retaliation

---

## Research foundation

The tool is based on research and practice from:

- psychological safety
- speak-up culture
- employee voice
- university ombuds systems
- ethics governance
- high-reliability organizations
- healthcare and aviation escalation practices
- non-retaliation and confidentiality policies

Important research ideas:

### Psychological safety

Psychological safety means people can raise concerns, mistakes, and risks without fear of humiliation or retaliation.

Important implication:

Teams with psychological safety often report more problems, not because they have more problems, but because people speak up earlier.

### Speak-up culture

Employees often remain silent for two main reasons:

1. Fear of negative consequences
2. Futility, meaning they believe speaking up will not change anything

A good escalation model must address both.

### Power asymmetry

In university settings, power asymmetries are often strong.

Examples:

- professor vs. PhD student
- supervisor vs. researcher
- committee chair vs. junior member
- tenured academic vs. temporary staff
- institutionally powerful stakeholder vs. administrative staff

The model must make it legitimate to bypass the direct line when the direct line is unsafe or conflicted.

### Confidentiality

Confidentiality means information is shared only with those who need it to handle the concern appropriately.

The model should make clear:

- what can stay informal
- what needs documentation
- who must be informed
- when confidentiality has limits

### Non-retaliation

People who raise concerns in good faith must not suffer disadvantages, exclusion, reputational harm, or career harm.

The model should include explicit anti-retaliation language.

### Independent channels

A team-owned model should not rely only on the hierarchy.

It should include independent or protected channels such as:

- ombuds office
- HR
- ethics office
- compliance office
- trusted contact
- external hotline
- whistleblowing channel
- union or staff representation

### High-reliability organizations

Healthcare and aviation use structured escalation methods to help people speak up despite hierarchy.

Useful concepts:

- PACE: Probe, Alert, Challenge, Escalate
- CUSS: Concerned, Uncomfortable, Safety issue, Stop
- Stop-the-line
- Crew Resource Management

These concepts show that escalation should be practiced, normalized, and supported by simple phrases.

### Under-escalation and over-escalation

The tool should explain both risks.

Under-escalation risk:

- serious concerns remain hidden
- misconduct continues
- employees are harmed
- ethical or legal risks grow
- trust is lost

Over-escalation risk:

- every issue becomes formal too early
- relationships are damaged
- formal channels are overloaded
- people avoid normal dialogue
- trust is also lost

A good model helps teams address issues at the lowest effective level while protecting safe routes for serious concerns.

---

## Design principles

The generated model should follow these principles:

1. Clarity  
   People know which level applies to which type of concern.

2. Safety  
   People can raise concerns without fear of retaliation.

3. Empowerment  
   Everyone has the right and responsibility to raise concerns.

4. Appropriateness  
   Issues are handled at the lowest effective level unless safety, ethics, legality, or power asymmetry require faster escalation.

5. Autonomy with alignment  
   Teams co-create their model, but it remains aligned with institutional policies and legal obligations.

6. Feedback and resolution  
   Escalation should have a follow-up path. People should know what happens after raising a concern.

7. De-escalation and recovery  
   After an issue is handled, the team should return to normal operations and restore trust.

8. Continuous improvement  
   The model should be reviewed regularly.

## Suggested app flow

### Step 1 — Start / Why this matters

Explain the tool in simple language.

Include this core message:

> In sensitive committees, escalation is not a sign of failure. It is a pre-agreed protection path when people, roles, or decisions come under pressure.

Include three learning points:

1. Psychological safety does not mean harmony. It means people can raise concerns, mistakes, and risks without fear of humiliation or retaliation.
2. In hierarchical environments, people often stay silent because they fear consequences or believe speaking up will not make a difference.
3. A good escalation model prevents both under-escalation and over-escalation.

Include a use case example:

> A university ethics committee handles sensitive cases involving professors, junior researchers, administrators, and confidential concerns. Because status and dependency can influence whether people speak up, the committee needs clear escalation levels and protected routes.

---

### Step 2 — Context Capture

Input fields:

- Team / committee name
- Type of sensitive situations handled
- Important power asymmetries
- People or roles needing protection
- Existing channels or resources
- What must not happen
- Review rhythm

Helper text:

> Describe the real context. The final model should fit your team, not an abstract policy.

---

### Step 3 — Concepts

Create compact concept cards.

Each card needs:

- short definition
- university-relevant example
- reflection question

Cards:

#### Psychological Safety

Definition:

People can raise concerns, mistakes, and risks without fear of humiliation or retaliation.

Example:

A junior researcher can question an ethical decision without being labelled difficult.

Question:

What would make people feel safe enough to speak here?

#### Speak-Up Culture

Definition:

Concerns are surfaced early and treated as contributions to quality, integrity, and safety.

Example:

A staff member raises a concern before a decision becomes formal.

Question:

Where do people currently stay silent?

#### Power Asymmetry

Definition:

Status, hierarchy, dependency, contracts, or academic authority can limit what people feel able to say.

Example:

A PhD student is reluctant to challenge a professor who influences their career.

Question:

Which power differences matter most in our context?

#### Confidentiality

Definition:

Information is shared only with people who need it to handle the concern appropriately.

Example:

A concern is discussed with a trusted contact before names are shared more widely.

Question:

What must remain confidential at each level?

#### Non-Retaliation

Definition:

People who raise concerns in good faith must not suffer disadvantages, exclusion, or career harm.

Example:

A person who reports pressure is protected from subtle retaliation.

Question:

What would retaliation look like in our setting?

#### Independent Channel

Definition:

A route outside the normal hierarchy when the hierarchy itself is part of the problem.

Example:

Ombuds office, ethics office, trusted external person, whistleblowing channel.

Question:

When is the direct line not safe enough?

#### Graded Assertiveness

Definition:

A structured way to escalate language step by step: Probe, Alert, Challenge, Escalate.

Example:

“I have a concern” → “I am uncomfortable” → “This may be unsafe or unethical” → “We need to stop and escalate.”

Question:

What phrases would help people speak up early?

#### De-escalation

Definition:

After the concern is handled, the team intentionally returns to normal working mode and restores trust.

Example:

After a formal clarification, the team debriefs and updates its process.

Question:

How do we close the loop after escalation?

### Step 4 — Escalation Levels Builder

Create a default 5-level model.

Users must be able to edit each level’s:

- name
- purpose
- triggers
- safe first step
- roles / contact path
- protection mechanisms
- documentation approach
- de-escalation criteria

Default levels:

#### Level 0 — Normal Mode

Purpose:

The issue can be handled in regular team dialogue.

Default trigger examples:

- ordinary disagreement
- minor misunderstanding
- process question
- clarification needed

Default safe step:

Discuss in the team or with the involved person, if safe.

Default protection:

Respectful dialogue, no blame, no unnecessary sharing.

---

#### Level 1 — Early Signal

Purpose:

A concern, irritation, or uncertainty becomes visible.

Default trigger examples:

- someone feels uncomfortable
- a concern is repeated
- there is uncertainty about authority, ethics, or confidentiality

Default safe step:

Raise the issue with a trusted colleague, chair, or designated contact.

Default protection:

Concern is acknowledged without pressure to formalize immediately.

---

#### Level 2 — Protected Consultation

Purpose:

A neutral or trusted person is involved without forcing formal escalation.

Default trigger examples:

- the direct line feels unsafe
- a professor, senior leader, or powerful stakeholder is involved
- the person fears negative consequences
- informal clarification is not appropriate

Default safe step:

Consult ombuds office, HR, ethics contact, trusted person, or neutral advisor.

Default protection:

Confidential consultation, need-to-know principle, no retaliation.

---

#### Level 3 — Formal Clarification

Purpose:

Formal governance, HR, leadership, compliance, ethics, or institutional structures are activated.

Default trigger examples:

- ethical, legal, safety, discrimination, harassment, or integrity concern
- repeated unresolved issue
- documented concern is ignored
- formal decision rights are unclear

Default safe step:

Document the concern and activate the appropriate formal route.

Default protection:

Clear documentation, role clarity, confidentiality, protection from retaliation.

---

#### Level 4 — Protection Mode

Purpose:

Immediate protective action, independent review, or crisis handling is required.

Default trigger examples:

- immediate harm or acute protection need
- retaliation risk
- severe misconduct
- internal channels are compromised
- institutional integrity or public trust is at risk

Default safe step:

Activate independent or senior institutional protection channel immediately.

Default protection:

Immediate safety measures, independent review, anti-retaliation monitoring, restricted access to sensitive information.

---

### Step 5 — Trigger Workshop

Create selectable trigger chips users can add to their model or final onepager:

- A person feels pressured
- Hierarchy prevents open discussion
- Confidentiality is at risk
- The direct reporting line is part of the problem
- A decision is pushed through despite documented concerns
- Staff fear negative consequences
- Several people report similar patterns
- A professor, senior leader, or influential stakeholder is involved
- Ethical, legal, safety, or reputational risk emerges
- Informal clarification failed
- The affected person asks for support
- Decision rights are unclear
- Retaliation risk exists
- Immediate protection is needed
- External or independent review may be necessary

Allow custom triggers.

---

### Step 6 — AI Prompt Builder

Create a visually distinct yellow AI section.

Text:

> AI does not decide. It helps the team reflect, sharpen language, identify blind spots, and prepare documentation.

Generate prompts dynamically from the current state and all user-entered level definitions.

Prompt modes:

1. Sharpen our model
2. Risk-review our model
3. Facilitate our workshop

---

#### Prompt mode A — Sharpen our model

```text
Act as an expert in organizational psychology, university governance, ethics committees, and psychologically safe escalation systems.

We are designing a team-owned protection and escalation model.

Context:
Team / committee: [team]
Sensitive situations: [situations]
Power asymmetries: [power]
People or roles needing protection: [protection]
Existing channels: [channels]
What must not happen: [red lines]
Review rhythm: [review rhythm]

Current escalation levels:
[include current levels with name, purpose, triggers, steps, roles, safeguards, documentation, de-escalation]

Please improve this model by:
1. Clarifying the purpose of each level
2. Sharpening the triggers
3. Strengthening protection mechanisms
4. Making power asymmetries more explicit
5. Adding de-escalation criteria
6. Suggesting wording that frames escalation as protection, not punishment

Use practical language suitable for a university/public-sector context.
```

---

#### Prompt mode B — Risk-review our model

```text
Act as a critical reviewer for a university escalation and employee-protection framework.

Review the following model for weaknesses.

Context:
[include all context fields]

Current model:
[include level definitions]

Please identify:
1. Where employees might still feel unsafe
2. Where power asymmetries are insufficiently addressed
3. Where confidentiality is unclear
4. Where retaliation risk is not sufficiently controlled
5. Where the model may lead to over-escalation
6. Where the model may lead to under-escalation
7. Missing independent channels
8. Missing documentation or feedback-loop mechanisms

Then suggest concrete improvements.
```

---

#### Prompt mode C — Facilitate our workshop

```text
Act as a facilitator for a 60-minute team workshop.

Design a practical workshop agenda to help this team co-create, test, or validate its protection and escalation model.

Context:
[include all context fields]

Current model:
[include level definitions]

Include:
- opening framing
- psychological safety setup
- concept introduction
- scenario exercise
- trigger mapping
- level refinement
- risk review
- final agreement
- closing and next steps

Add facilitator notes for handling hierarchy, silence, fear of consequences, and sensitive cases.
```

Create a live-generated output titled:

Sections:

1. Purpose
2. Context
3. Guiding principles
4. Escalation levels
5. Key triggers
6. Roles and contact paths
7. Protection mechanisms
8. Documentation and confidentiality
9. De-escalation and recovery
10. Review rhythm
11. Open questions

Default guiding principles:

- Escalation is a protection path, not a punishment path.
- Concerns raised in good faith are treated as contributions to safety and integrity.
- Issues should be handled at the lowest effective level, unless safety, ethics, legality, or power asymmetry require faster escalation.
- Confidentiality follows a need-to-know principle.
- Retaliation is not acceptable.
- Independent channels are legitimate when the normal hierarchy is unsafe or conflicted.
- Every escalation should have a path back to clarification, learning, and trust.

Buttons:

- Copy Onepager
- Copy as Markdown

- Reset all data

## Important implementation notes

Keep the first version small.

The concept cards should educate, but not dominate the interface.

The level builder and final onepager are the core.

The AI section should feel like support embedded in the workflow, not like a chatbot.

Avoid overbuilding:

- no login
- no backend
- no database
- no multi-page system
- no complex project management
- no long program timeline
- no analytics

This is a compact guided HTML workbench.
